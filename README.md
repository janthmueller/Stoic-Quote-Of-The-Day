<p align="center">
    <em><!--START_SECTION:quote-text-->
It takes the whole of life to learn how to live, and — what will perhaps make you wonder more — it takes the whole of life to learn how to die.
<!--END_SECTION:quote-text--></em><br>
    <strong>— <!--START_SECTION:quote-author-->
Seneca
<!--END_SECTION:quote-author--></strong>
</p>

<div align="center" style="max-width:600px;margin:0 auto;">
<!--START_SECTION:quote-interpretation-->
The words of Seneca remind us that life is an endless journey of growth and discovery, challenging the notion that understanding should come at a certain age or stage. In modern life, we often feel pressure to have everything figured out quickly—whether it's knowing exactly what career path to choose, when to start a family, or how to find happiness. Yet, Seneca's wisdom suggests that living fully requires a lifetime of learning because life's complexities continually evolve, and so do we. Each experience, pleasant or otherwise, contributes to our understanding of who we are and what brings meaning to our existence. Moreover, contemplating our mortality, as unsettling as it might be, forms an integral part of living well. Recognizing that our time is finite can deepen our appreciation for the present and motivate us to invest in what truly matters—relationships, personal growth, and kindness. Embracing the uncertainty and perpetual learning that both life and death encompass can guide us to live sincerely and prepare us to depart with peace. In essence, life's greatest lessons lie in its ongoing nature, inviting us to continually adapt and reflect until our journey earns its final punctuation.
<!--END_SECTION:quote-interpretation-->
