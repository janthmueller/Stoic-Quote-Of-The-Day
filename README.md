    <em><!--START_SECTION:quote-text-->
It is sweet to draw the world down with you when you are perishing.
<!--END_SECTION:quote-text--></em><br>
    <strong>— <!--START_SECTION:quote-author-->
Seneca
<!--END_SECTION:quote-author--></strong>
</p>

<div align="center" style="max-width:600px;margin:0 auto;">
<!--START_SECTION:quote-interpretation-->
When Seneca reflects on the temptation to bring others down when one's life or situation is crumbling, he is highlighting a common psychological impulse: the desire for shared suffering. It can feel comforting to know that others are similarly struggling, as it diminishes the isolation of personal pain. This tendency often appears as people navigate challenging times, whether it be through workplace difficulties, personal crises, or even witnessing the turbulent events of the world. In such moments, there is a powerful allure in persuading others that things are equally dire for them, almost like finding solace in collective misery. However, this inclination toward shared despair can divert attention from more constructive responses to adversity—like resilience, personal growth, and seeking solutions that elevate rather than entangle others in negativity. Instead of succumbing to this impulse, recognizing and regulating it may lead to healthier interactions and personal empowerment, even amid life's inevitable downfalls. For instance, at work, instead of rallying colleagues into a spiral of complaints during a stressful project, it is more beneficial to foster a supportive environment where challenges are acknowledged and addressed, allowing for collective action towards improvement. This way, not only does one resist the temptation to pull others down, but also helps cultivate a space where optimism and collaboration can thrive, transforming individual struggles into opportunities for shared success.
