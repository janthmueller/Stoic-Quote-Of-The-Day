<p align="center">
    <em><!--START_SECTION:quote-text-->
Neither worse then nor better is a thing made by being praised.
<!--END_SECTION:quote-text--></em><br>
    <strong>— <!--START_SECTION:quote-author-->
Marcus Aurelius
<!--END_SECTION:quote-author--></strong>
</p>

<div align="center" style="max-width:600px;margin:0 auto;">
<!--START_SECTION:quote-interpretation-->
Our perception of value is often clouded by external opinions and accolades, but true worth remains unchanged by such judgments, a sentiment shared by Marcus Aurelius. In today’s context, consider a talented artist displaying their work. When praised, their art doesn't become inherently better; similarly, if criticized, it isn't less valuable. This insight invites us to find contentment and assurance in our own evaluation of things, rather than constantly seeking validation through external praise or criticism. Social media accentuates this idea, where posts and images are frequently measured by likes and comments, but the core essence of what is posted isn't transformed by these online reactions. The Stoics remind us that seeking approval is fleeting and unreliable for gauging the true quality of our endeavors, encouraging a focus on intrinsic values and the personal satisfaction derived from our actions and creations. By understanding that praise is transient and subjective, we cultivate personal resilience and a stable sense of self-worth rooted in our own standards and contributions, rather than dancing to the tune of others' opinions.
